Marie Curie

Marie Salomea Curie, née Skłodowska, was a physicist and chemist, famous for her research on radioactivity. She was born in Warsaw, Poland on November 7, 1867, and later immigrated to Paris, France where she studied Physics and the Mathematical Sciences. Her work with her husband and research partner, Pierre Curie, led to the isolation of polonium and radium. Pierre died in 1906 in a road accident.

Marie Curie became a member of the Conseil du Physique Solvay in 1911, and a member of the Committee of intellectual Co-operation of the League of Nations in 1922. During her life she published many scientific papers and three books; Recherches sur les Substances Radioactives (Research on Radioactive Substances), L’Isotopie et les Éléments Isotopes (The Isotope and Isotopic Elements), and Traité’ de Radioactivité (Treatise on Radioactivity).

Marie died on July 4, 1934 from aplastic anemia, possibly resulting from bone marrow damage caused by her long-term exposure to radiation.

Some of her many acollades include:

  • Doctor of Science - 1903
  • Nobel Prize in Physics - 1903 (jointly awarded with Pierre Curie)
  • Davy Medal of the Royal Society - 1903 (jointly awarded with Pierre Curie)
  • First woman to be Head of the Physics Laboratory at the Sorbonne - 1906
  • Nobel Prize in Chemistry - 1911
  • Director of the Radium Institute at the University of Paris - 1914

From Nobel Lectures, Physics 1901-1921, Elsevier Publishing Company, Amsterdam, 1967.
